## Summary
A fresh debate over “government by algorithm” has moved from theory to implementation timelines. The UAE’s push to have 50% of federal operations powered by agentic AI within two years sharpens a core democratic question: efficiency gains are real, but legitimacy depends on whether citizens can audit, contest, and reverse automated state decisions.

## What Happened
- Project Syndicate contributors Gabriela Ramos and Emilija Stojmenova Duh argued this week that delegating public decisions to AI systems can weaken accountability when transparency and oversight lag deployment.
- MIT Sloan Management Review Middle East reported the UAE directive to target 50% of federal operations with agentic AI, framing AI as an "executive partner" in government workflows.
- Together, those developments indicate that policy institutions are no longer debating distant hypotheticals; they are confronting near-term operational choices.

## Opinion
Governments should treat agentic AI the way constitutional systems treat any concentration of power: as something that must be constrained before it is scaled.

Public agencies can and should use machine learning for triage, forecasting, and service routing. But delegating final administrative judgment to systems that are difficult to explain creates a rights problem, not just a technical one. Citizens need clear notice when AI materially shaped a decision, meaningful appeal routes to a human official, and auditable records that courts and watchdogs can inspect.

The best test is simple: if an agency cannot explain a decision path in plain language to the affected person, that system is not ready for high-stakes use. Speed without intelligibility is not modernization; it is opacity at machine scale.

## Why It Matters
For opinion-editorials readers, this is a governance stress test likely to spread beyond one country. As states race to automate public administration, the policy frontier is shifting from “Can we deploy?” to “What must be non-negotiable before deployment is legitimate?”
