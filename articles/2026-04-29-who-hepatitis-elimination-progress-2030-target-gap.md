## Summary
The World Health Organization says global efforts against viral hepatitis have reduced new hepatitis B infections and hepatitis C deaths since 2015, but progress remains too slow to meet 2030 elimination targets.

## What Happened
In a report released at the World Hepatitis Summit, WHO said viral hepatitis B and C caused about 1.34 million deaths in 2024, while transmission still runs at roughly 1.8 million new infections per year. WHO reported that annual new hepatitis B infections are down 32% since 2015 and hepatitis C-related deaths are down 12%, but warned that current momentum is uneven across regions and populations.

WHO highlighted major treatment and prevention gaps, including low hepatitis B birth-dose coverage in parts of Africa and limited treatment access for people living with chronic hepatitis B or C. The UN News service separately reported WHO's call for faster action, emphasizing the agency's warning that missed diagnoses and treatment delays are still driving preventable deaths.

## Why It Matters
This is a science-health story because it directly concerns global disease-burden trends, vaccination coverage, treatment access, and public-health system performance. Whether countries accelerate testing, prevention, and treatment now will shape whether the 2030 elimination targets are achievable.
