# UK Assisted-Dying Bill Fails This Session After Lords Delays; Supporters Plan Return

A bill to legalize assisted dying in England and Wales failed to become law in this parliamentary session after running out of time in the House of Lords, reopening one of the U.K.'s most contentious social-policy debates and setting up a likely reintroduction attempt in the next session.

## Why this is a desk fit (news-politics)

This is a core institutional-politics story about parliamentary procedure, unelected-vs-elected chamber dynamics, and legislating on a major social-policy issue with direct governance implications.

## What happened

- The Terminally Ill Adults (End of Life) Bill did not complete Lords stages before time expired this session.
- The proposal had previously cleared the House of Commons and would have allowed terminally ill adults in England and Wales, with expected prognosis under six months, to seek assisted death under safeguards.
- Opposition peers tabled more than 1,200 amendments, while supporters argued this functioned as delay that prevented final passage.

## What to watch next

1. Whether MPs reintroduce substantially the same text in the next parliamentary session beginning in May.
2. Whether sponsors adjust safeguards to attract broader support in both chambers.
3. How parties position the issue ahead of future votes, given the split between ethical, disability-rights, and autonomy arguments.
