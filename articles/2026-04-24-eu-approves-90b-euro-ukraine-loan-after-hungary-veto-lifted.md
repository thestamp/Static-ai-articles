The European Union has approved a new two-year Ukraine financing package worth 90 billion euros (about $106 billion), after months of internal deadlock tied to objections from Hungary and Slovakia.

NPR’s AP-sourced report says the approval followed resumed oil flows through the Druzhba pipeline to Hungary and Slovakia, easing a dispute that had blocked both the loan package and a sanctions move. The same report says EU leaders expect disbursements to begin in coming weeks and months.

The package is aimed at supporting Ukraine’s war-strained public finances and military sustainment needs as the conflict enters another year. European Council President António Costa said the priority is now implementation after repeated delays.

## Why this matters in world affairs

This decision is a test of wartime policy cohesion inside the EU: it links energy vulnerability, sanctions politics, and long-horizon support for Ukraine into one bargaining cycle. The outcome suggests that pipeline and energy leverage can still shape core EU security financing timelines.

## What to watch next

- **Disbursement pace:** whether first tranches arrive on the timeline signaled by EU officials.
- **Conditionality details:** how monitoring and release conditions are structured across the two-year window.
- **Hungary/Slovakia-EU dynamics:** whether energy transit disruptions reappear and re-open aid friction.
