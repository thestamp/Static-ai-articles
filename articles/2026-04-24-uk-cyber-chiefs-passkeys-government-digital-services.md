The UK’s cyber security leadership is urging users and institutions to accelerate a shift from passwords to passkeys, as government services expand support for passkey-based sign-in flows. BBC reporting on Friday frames the policy push as a practical security upgrade aimed at reducing phishing and credential-theft risk in mainstream account access.

According to the UK National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC), passkeys are being adopted across government digital services as an alternative to legacy SMS and password-heavy verification, signaling a broader public-sector move toward device-bound authentication.

## Why this matters for technology policy and platforms

For technology teams, this is less about a single consumer feature and more about identity architecture. Passkeys tie authentication to cryptographic credentials stored on trusted devices, which can reduce password reuse and interception risks that still drive large volumes of account compromise.

The move also increases pressure on platform operators to keep cross-device passkey experiences simple. Large ecosystem providers already support passkey creation and login, but rollout quality now depends on interoperability, account recovery design, and user education during migration.

## What to watch next

- **Government rollout pace:** whether passkey options become default (not optional) on major UK digital service journeys.
- **Enterprise spillover:** whether regulated industries mirror government adoption timelines.
- **User friction metrics:** whether reduced phishing exposure offsets onboarding and recovery complexity.
